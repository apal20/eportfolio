Seminar 2 - TCP/IP vs ISO/OSI

Transmission Control Protocol, developed gradually by the IETF (originating from ARPA to IAB), without the kings, presidents and voting, through rough consensus of the participants and running codes, stood far ahead against the International Standards Organisation’s Open Systems Interconnection (OSI).

The bureaucratic governance of the OSI resembled contemporary democartic bodies in so far as it featured voting, partisan compromises, and rule making behaviour designed to protect financial interests. These international standards were written as documents to be obeyed and enjoyed this widespread support from national governments particularly in Western Europe, North America, and Far East. OSI enjoyed this level of support due to the strategic position of its sponsor, ISO.

In contrast to it, Transmission Control Protocol was developed by the network users – technical experts distributed in universities, firms and government agencies. These Internet standards tended to be those written for implementers. The ulitmate goal was to make the network transparent, “a seamless whole”, invisible to the user who would be more interested in accessing information over the network instead of the operation of the network itself. Network Transparency and Application Autonomy were the two key elements behind the Internet Protocols. The end-to-end design principal, calls for a simple standard for data transfer allowing new innovations to be added at the edges and on top of the “stupid network”.

Considering the modern Internet, where we are surrounded by the mobile applications, social media, ecommerce, instant messaging platforms, VoIP, wireless networking technologies such as WiFi and mobile data, it seems that no user has any idea about how the “stupid network” is working and is using the end-to-end deisgn principle to transfer the data from one node to another. A user coming from a low income background with minimum to no knowledge of modern computing and technology, is able to operate a mobile handset easily and use all the service available and access the vast information available on the Internet.

From the article, it is evident that the proprieratory networks established by the IBM and DEC stood no chance against the open networks. In general, the open systems encourage compatibility between systems from different manufacturers. Adoption of these open systems, no matter if they came from a tech giant such as Microsoft or from the Open Source groups such as that led for the development of the Linux, provided the developers to build web applications that can be used on any browser, or accessed on any platform. This in turn provided the users a freedom to choose the applications and device of their own choice while able to connect to the internet without any hassle. In modern times, these systems provided a seamless connection between mobile devices or desktop / laptops and hide the underlying layers from the users.

It is highly evident from above that the TCP/IP has made the world a better place and no matter how many governments or organisations laid out the rules for the inter-connection of the devices. The systems developed by the Rough Consensus and Running Code paved a path for the future. With IPv6 being arrived and exponential increase in number of devices (Internet of Things / Machine to Machine communication), TCP/IP will provide a very solid foundation.
