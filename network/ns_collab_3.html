Collaborative Discussion 3 - GDPR Case Study

Initial Post

I have chosen the eleventh case study in the year 2017 which is 'Failure by the Department of Justice and Equality to impose the correct access restrictions on access to medical data of an employee'.

In this article, a report containing information on the employee's health had been accidentally uploaded to a general departmental open document management database by the line manager in 2012, which then remained on that database for up to three years where it could be accessed by approximately 80 employees. The employee had requested an explanation from the Department as to why the report had been placed on an open database but had not received official confirmation that the report had since been removed for which the department confirmed that notes relating to a discussion which had taken place between the complainant and their line manager in 2012 (which included a note concerning the complainant’s health) had been stored to the database in question and marked private. However, the line manager had inadvertently omitted to restrict access to the document with the result that it could be accessed by approximately 80 staff members from the Department. They later informed that the document had been removed from the database some three years ago after being saved on the computer. As the line manager in question had since left the Department, it had been unable to establish exactly why the document had been saved there in the first place but claimed that it was due to human error.At the end, the department was also unable to establish how many staff had actually accessed the document during those three years in which it was accessible as the department’s IT section had been unable to restore the historic data in question. At last, the department made an offer, as a way of resolving the matter to the employee in writing by confirming that the document in question had been removed from the database and apologising for any distress caused. But, the employee chose not to accept this offer and instead sought a formal decision of the Commissioner.In her decision, the Commissioner concluded that the Department had contravened Section 2A(1) and 2B(1) of the Data Protection Acts 1988 & 2003 by processing the complainant’s sensitive personal data without the required consent or another valid legal basis for doing so and by disclosing the complainant’s sensitive personal data to at least one third party.

The GDPR defines an array of legal terms at length. Below are some of the most important ones that are referred to, in this article:

Personal data — Personal data is any information that relates to an individual who can be directly or indirectly identified. Names and email addresses are obviously personal data. Location information, ethnicity, gender, biometric data, religious beliefs, web cookies, and political opinions can also be personal data.

Data processing — Any action performed on data, whether automated or manual. The examples cited in the text include collecting, recording, organising, structuring, storing, using, erasing, etc.

Data processor — A third party that processes personal data on behalf of a data controller.

Data controller — The person who decides why and how personal data will be processed, i.e an owner or employee in your organisation who handles data.

Data subject — The person whose data is processed, such as customers and site visitors.

If I was the information Security manager in this organisation, then I would have had adequate regular audit and review measures in place for evaluating the appropriateness of documents stored to open access databases as well as adequate system of training and ensuring awareness by staff managers of basic data protection rules in order to solve to mitigate the issue.

Summary Post

In this collaborative discussion, we reviewed a number of case studies involving GDPR investigations undertaken by the Data Protection Commission (2020). Many of these case studies involved data subject access requests (DSARs), some involved accidental data breaches and loss while others focussed on the peculiarities of non electronic forms of data which are not subject to electronic encryption.

For my case study, I selected the case study titled "Failure by the Department of Justice and Equality to impose the correct access restrictions on access to medical data of an employee." Due to the human error by the department's personnel, the medical report information of an employee was wrongly posted on an open access site accessed by other employees, the act violated the Data Protection Act of 1988 and 2003. The Act controls how employers acquire, store, and utilize the personal information of their workers. I  believe that a company's information security system should contain data/security alerts, a defined retention policy for all sensitive personal data, and regular audit of the procedures for storing of datasets and proper information security training for employees.

In response to my post, Jubet (2022)made some important observations related to the human factor as a cause of breach, to quote: “The human factor in security breaches is a very important factor to consider in evaluating the effectiveness of any security measure. The question an IT professional should ask is not just “which security solution do we like best” but “which security solution best factors in human behavior”(Wang, 2014). Regular staff training to educate them on Data protection should also be a common denominator of all security measures.” He also pointed out that there are response tools such Data Loss Prevention (DLP) which can monitor and gather information from systems logs, identify sensitive data and ensure it is not lost or leaked, while also setting remediation processes for responsible parties, to remediate these potential breaches within a time frame(Imperva.com, N.D).  

According to  J Jubet., (2022) data breaches are happening at an alarming rate following the increasing awareness among cyber criminals of the value of personal data. This issue was highlighted by the missing evidence case study which may lead to a person losing their court case and could lead to other more serious complications if the data was illegally accessed by cyber criminals. The far reaching implication of this type of breach calls for stringent and fool proof measures of data protection to be employed and at the same time have inbuilt mechanisms to allow appropriate review/audit of these methods from time to time.

E Cook., (2022) highlighted the challenge with none electronic forms of data which cannot be protected by encryption and access controls. Cook also advised that a control measure such as an audit process to sign in and out the evidence materials should be enforced to avoid a repeat of this type of data breach (Cook, 2022).

Jonathan’s A.,(2022) case study examines the use of CCTV video in a disciplinary proceeding involving a security officer who was tasked to monitor the CCTV system at night. The pre-GDPR complaint was filed under the Data Protection Acts 1988-2003 of the Republic of Ireland (Data Protection Commission, 2018). I feel that legal safeguards should be in place for the controller's or a third party's legitimate interests unless those interests are outweighed by the data subject's interests or fundamental rights and freedoms.
