Collaborative Discussion 3 - GDPR Case Study

Summary Post

In this collaborative discussion, we reviewed a number of case studies involving GDPR investigations undertaken by the Data Protection Commission (2020). Many of these case studies involved data subject access requests (DSARs), some involved accidental data breaches and loss while others focussed on the peculiarities of non electronic forms of data which are not subject to electronic encryption.

For my case study, I selected the case study titled "Failure by the Department of Justice and Equality to impose the correct access restrictions on access to medical data of an employee." Due to the human error by the department's personnel, the medical report information of an employee was wrongly posted on an open access site accessed by other employees, the act violated the Data Protection Act of 1988 and 2003. The Act controls how employers acquire, store, and utilize the personal information of their workers. I  believe that a company's information security system should contain data/security alerts, a defined retention policy for all sensitive personal data, and regular audit of the procedures for storing of datasets and proper information security training for employees.

In response to my post, Jubet (2022)made some important observations related to the human factor as a cause of breach, to quote: “The human factor in security breaches is a very important factor to consider in evaluating the effectiveness of any security measure. The question an IT professional should ask is not just “which security solution do we like best” but “which security solution best factors in human behavior”(Wang, 2014). Regular staff training to educate them on Data protection should also be a common denominator of all security measures.” He also pointed out that there are response tools such Data Loss Prevention (DLP) which can monitor and gather information from systems logs, identify sensitive data and ensure it is not lost or leaked, while also setting remediation processes for responsible parties, to remediate these potential breaches within a time frame(Imperva.com, N.D).  

According to  J Jubet., (2022) data breaches are happening at an alarming rate following the increasing awareness among cyber criminals of the value of personal data. This issue was highlighted by the missing evidence case study which may lead to a person losing their court case and could lead to other more serious complications if the data was illegally accessed by cyber criminals. The far reaching implication of this type of breach calls for stringent and fool proof measures of data protection to be employed and at the same time have inbuilt mechanisms to allow appropriate review/audit of these methods from time to time.

E Cook., (2022) highlighted the challenge with none electronic forms of data which cannot be protected by encryption and access controls. Cook also advised that a control measure such as an audit process to sign in and out the evidence materials should be enforced to avoid a repeat of this type of data breach (Cook, 2022).

Jonathan’s A.,(2022) case study examines the use of CCTV video in a disciplinary proceeding involving a security officer who was tasked to monitor the CCTV system at night. The pre-GDPR complaint was filed under the Data Protection Acts 1988-2003 of the Republic of Ireland (Data Protection Commission, 2018). I feel that legal safeguards should be in place for the controller's or a third party's legitimate interests unless those interests are outweighed by the data subject's interests or fundamental rights and freedoms.
